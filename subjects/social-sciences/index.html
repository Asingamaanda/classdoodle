Chapter 1: The Atlantic Slave Trade ‚Äì One of History's Darkest Chapters üïØÔ∏è

Content Warning: This chapter discusses slavery, a deeply painful period in human history. We approach this topic with respect for those who suffered and to learn from the past so we never repeat these horrors.

What Was the Atlantic Slave Trade?

Between the 1500s and 1800s, approximately 12.5 MILLION African people were forcibly taken from their homes, transported across the Atlantic Ocean in horrific conditions, and sold into slavery in the Americas.

üìä The Numbers Tell a Story

  • 12.5 million enslaved Africans transported
  • ~2 million died during the Middle Passage (the ocean journey)
  • 400+ years of this brutal trade
  • 3 continents involved: Africa, Europe, Americas
  • Destroyed countless African kingdoms and communities

üé• Atlantic Slave Trade Overview (12 min)

1. The Triangular Trade Route üî∫

The slave trade formed a "triangle" across the Atlantic Ocean with three legs:

üü£ LEG 1: Europe to Africa

European ships sailed to Africa loaded with:

  • Guns and ammunition
  • Cloth and textiles
  • Alcohol (rum, wine)
  • Metal goods (pots, tools)
  • Beads and trinkets

Purpose: Trade these goods for enslaved people

üî¥ LEG 2: The Middle Passage (Africa to Americas)

THE MOST HORRIFIC JOURNEY IN HISTORY

  • Enslaved Africans packed into ship holds like cargo
  • Chained together in tiny spaces - couldn't stand or turn
  • Journey took 6-8 weeks
  • No sanitation, little food/water
  • Disease was rampant (dysentery, smallpox)
  • 15-20% died during the voyage
  • Bodies thrown overboard

Survivors: Arrived traumatized, sick, and separated from families forever

üü¢ LEG 3: Americas to Europe

Ships returned to Europe loaded with products made by enslaved people:

  • Sugar - from Caribbean plantations
  • Cotton - from American South
  • Tobacco - from Virginia
  • Coffee - from Brazil
  • Rum - distilled from sugar

Result: Massive wealth for European merchants and plantation owners

2. Life on the Plantations üåæ

Once in the Americas, enslaved people were sold at slave auctions and forced to work on large farms called plantations.

‚òÄÔ∏è Daily Life

  • Worked from sunrise to sunset (12-16 hours/day)
  • Hard labor: planting, harvesting, processing crops
  • Hot, dangerous conditions (sugar mills, cotton fields)
  • Minimal food - mainly corn, beans, scraps
  • Lived in tiny, crowded cabins with dirt floors
  • No pay - they were considered "property"

‚ö†Ô∏è Brutal Punishment System

  • Whipping for "disobedience" or working "too slowly"
  • Shackles and chains
  • Family separation as punishment
  • Branding with hot irons
  • Death for attempting escape

Goal: Break their spirit, prevent resistance, maximize profit

‚úä Resistance & Survival

Despite the horrors, enslaved people NEVER stopped resisting:

  • Everyday resistance: Working slowly, breaking tools, pretending not to understand
  • Running away: Escaping to freedom (Underground Railroad in USA)
  • Rebellions: Armed uprisings (Haiti Revolution 1791-1804 = SUCCESS!)
  • Preserving culture: Secret songs, dances, religions, stories
  • Building community: Supporting each other, protecting children

üí° Remember:

Enslaved people were NOT passive victims. They were fighters, survivors, strategists. Their resistance eventually contributed to slavery's end.

3. Why Did It Happen? And How Did It End? ü§î

‚ö†Ô∏è Why Did the Slave Trade Happen?

1. üí∞ Economic Greed

European demand for sugar, tobacco, cotton EXPLODED. Plantations needed MASSIVE labor forces. Slavery = "free" labor = HUGE profits.

2. üß† Racist Ideology

Europeans created FALSE theories that Africans were "inferior" or "less human" to justify slavery. This racism was taught in schools, churches, laws.

3. ‚öîÔ∏è Military/Technology Advantage

Europeans had guns, ships, and played African kingdoms against each other. Some African leaders participated (forced or paid) in capturing people.

4. ‚õ™ Religious Justification

Some (not all) Christian leaders misused Bible verses to claim slavery was "God's will." Many others opposed this and fought against slavery.

‚úä The Abolition Movement (How It Ended)

Slavery didn't end on its own - it took hundreds of years of brave resistance from enslaved people AND allies who fought for freedom:

üì£ Abolitionists (Anti-Slavery Activists)

  • Olaudah Equiano - Former slave, wrote powerful autobiography exposing horrors
  • William Wilberforce - British politician, campaigned for 20 years
  • Harriet Tubman - Escaped slave, rescued 300+ people via Underground Railroad
  • Frederick Douglass - Brilliant speaker and writer, exposed slavery's evils
  • Sojourner Truth - Powerful activist for abolition and women's rights

üìú Key Milestones

  • 1791-1804: Haitian Revolution - enslaved people WON independence! First Black republic!
  • 1807: Britain banned the slave TRADE (not slavery yet)
  • 1833: Britain abolished slavery in colonies
  • 1865: USA abolished slavery (after Civil War)
  • 1888: Brazil - last country in Americas to abolish slavery

üåü What Led to Change?

  • Slave rebellions made slavery costly and dangerous
  • Economic changes - Industrial Revolution reduced need for slave labor
  • Moral pressure - Growing numbers saw slavery as evil
  • Political activism - Petitions, boycotts, protests
  • Religious movements - Quakers and others led opposition

4. The Lasting Impact (Why It Still Matters Today) üåç

The slave trade ended over 130 years ago, but its effects are STILL felt today:

üí∞ Economic Impact

  • Wealth gap: Europe/Americas built wealth on slave labor; Africa lost millions of workers
  • Underdevelopment: African kingdoms weakened, economies destroyed
  • Generational poverty: Formerly enslaved families started with NOTHING after freedom

üë• Social Impact

  • Racism: Ideas used to justify slavery still cause prejudice today
  • Stereotypes: Harmful false beliefs about Black people persist
  • Inequality: Descendants of enslaved people face ongoing discrimination

üéµ Cultural Impact

  • Diaspora: Millions of African descendants now live in Americas
  • Cultural fusion: African culture shaped music (jazz, blues, reggae), food, language
  • Lost heritage: Enslaved people couldn't trace ancestors, languages lost

üí° Lessons for Today

  • ‚ùå NEVER dehumanize any group - it's how atrocities begin
  • ‚úä Speak up against injustice - silence helps oppressors
  • üìö Learn history honestly - we can't fix what we don't acknowledge
  • ü§ù Work for equality - effects of slavery aren't over
  • üåü Celebrate resistance - honor those who fought for freedom

Chapter 2: The Industrial Revolution ‚Äì The World Changed Forever ‚öôÔ∏è

What Was the Industrial Revolution?

Starting around 1760 in Britain, the world transformed from making things by hand to making them with MACHINES. This period (1760-1840) is called the Industrial Revolution - and it changed EVERYTHING about how humans lived, worked, and organized society.

üîÑ Before vs After

üï∞Ô∏è BEFORE (1700s)

  • Most people = farmers
  • Lived in small villages
  • Handmade goods (slow!)
  • Limited travel
  • Life expectancy: ~35 years

‚ö° AFTER (1840s)

  • Most people = factory workers
  • Lived in crowded cities
  • Machine-made goods (fast!)
  • Trains & steamships
  • Life expectancy: ~40 years (slowly improving)

üì∫ Watch: Industrial Revolution Overview

üé• Industrial Revolution Explained (10 min)

1. Revolutionary Inventions & Machines ‚öôÔ∏è

New inventions = the ENGINE of the Industrial Revolution. Each breakthrough made production faster, cheaper, and changed society:

üî• Steam Engine (1712, improved by James Watt 1769)

THE game-changer!

  • Used coal to heat water ‚Üí steam ‚Üí power!
  • Powered factories, trains, ships
  • No longer dependent on water/wind for power
  • Made Britain the world's first industrial superpower

üßµ Textile Revolution (1760s-1780s)

Machines transformed cloth-making from cottage industry to factory system:

  • Spinning Jenny: 1 person could spin 8 threads (later 120!)
  • Water Frame: Used water power, made stronger thread
  • Spinning Mule: Combined best of both = fine, strong thread
  • Power Loom: Automated weaving, 40x faster than hand-weaving!

Result: Britain became world's #1 textile exporter!

üöÇ Railways (1825 onwards)

  • George Stephenson's "Locomotion No. 1" = first public railway (1825)
  • Speed: 15 mph (vs walking 3 mph, horse 8 mph)
  • Connected cities, transported goods & people FAST
  • By 1850: 6,000 miles of track in Britain!
  • Created jobs: miners, engineers, station workers

2. The Factory System & Rise of Cities üè≠

üè≠ What Were Factories?

Instead of workers making products at home (cottage industry), factories brought hundreds of workers + machines under ONE roof.

‚úÖ Advantages (for Factory Owners)

  • Mass production: Make THOUSANDS of items/day
  • Cheaper goods: Machines faster than handwork
  • Control: Supervise workers easily
  • Division of labor: Each worker does ONE task (faster!)
  • HUGE profits!

‚ùå Disadvantages (for Workers)

  • Dangerous: Machines had NO safety guards - limbs often crushed
  • Long hours: 12-16 hour days, 6 days/week
  • Low wages: Barely enough to survive
  • Repetitive: Same task all day = boring, soul-crushing
  • Unhealthy: Poor ventilation, toxic fumes, loud noise
  • No job security: Fired easily if injured or sick

üåÜ Urbanization (Rural ‚Üí City Migration)

Millions of people moved from farms to cities for factory jobs. Cities EXPLODED in size:

üìä Population Explosion

Manchester (textile city):

1750: 18,000 people
1850: 303,000!

London:

1750: 750,000
1850: 2.5 MILLION!

üèöÔ∏è Living Conditions in Cities (AWFUL!)

  • Overcrowding: Entire families in ONE room
  • No sanitation: No toilets, sewage in streets
  • Polluted water: Spread cholera, typhoid (killed thousands)
  • Air pollution: Coal smoke = "pea soup" fog, lung disease
  • Crime: Poverty led to theft, violence
  • No education: Kids worked instead of school

üí° Why Did People Still Come?

Despite horrible conditions, cities offered:

  • Jobs (farms had less work due to machines)
  • Cash wages (not just food from farming)
  • Hope for better life
  • Escape from rural poverty

3. Child Labor ‚Äì The Cruelest Reality üë∂üíî

Children as young as 5 years old worked in factories, mines, and mills. This was one of the darkest aspects of the Industrial Revolution.

üò¢ Why Did Factory Owners Use Children?

  • Cheap: Paid 1/10th of adult wages
  • Small: Could crawl under machines to fix threads
  • Obedient: Less likely to complain or strike
  • Nimble fingers: Good for detailed work
  • Desperate families: Needed children's income to survive

üè≠ What Jobs Did Children Do?

  • Textile mills: Tied broken threads, cleaned machines (while running!)
  • Coal mines: Pulled coal carts through tiny tunnels
  • Chimney sweeps: Climbed inside chimneys to clean soot (got stuck, suffocated)
  • Match factories: Made matches - phosphorus poisoning caused "phossy jaw" (jaw rotted away!)

üíî Daily Life of Child Workers

  • Work hours: 12-16 hours/day, starting at 5am
  • Injuries: Lost fingers, limbs in machines; lung disease from dust
  • Beatings: Hit if they fell asleep or worked "too slowly"
  • Stunted growth: Malnutrition + overwork = many grew up deformed
  • No education: Couldn't read or write - trapped in poverty
  • Early death: Many died before age 20 from accidents or disease

‚úä The Fight for Reform (How It Changed)

Brave activists, journalists, and politicians fought back:

1833 - Factory Act (Britain)

‚Ä¢ No children under 9 in textile factories
‚Ä¢ Ages 9-13: Max 9 hours/day
‚Ä¢ Ages 13-18: Max 12 hours/day
‚Ä¢ 2 hours school/day required

1842 - Mines Act

‚Ä¢ Banned women & children under 10 from underground mining
‚Ä¢ Factory inspectors appointed to enforce laws

1874 - Factory Act

‚Ä¢ Minimum age raised to 10
‚Ä¢ Max 10 hours/day for under-18s
‚Ä¢ Half-day on Saturdays

1880 - Education Act

‚Ä¢ School became COMPULSORY ages 5-10
‚Ä¢ Children had to be in school, NOT factories!

üí° Why It Matters: These laws didn't happen overnight - they took 50+ years of protests, reports, and political pressure. Change is possible, but requires persistent action!

4. Impact & Legacy (Still Affects Us Today!) üåç

The Industrial Revolution didn't just change the 1800s - it shaped the ENTIRE modern world:

üí∞ Economic Impact

  • Capitalism: Private ownership, profit motive, competition
  • Global trade: Railways + steamships = connected world
  • Wealth gap: Factory owners became super-rich; workers stayed poor (led to class conflict)
  • Modern economy: Banking, stock markets, insurance all grew

üë• Social Changes

  • New social classes: Middle class (managers, professionals) & working class (factory workers)
  • Labor unions: Workers organized to demand rights, better pay
  • Socialism/Communism: Karl Marx wrote "Communist Manifesto" (1848) demanding worker ownership
  • Public education: Governments realized educated workers = better economy
  • Women's work: Many women worked in factories (though paid less than men)

üî¨ Technological Revolution

  • Innovation culture: "If we can imagine it, we can build it!"
  • Scientific method: Applied to engineering, medicine
  • Foundation for 2nd Industrial Revolution: Electricity, cars, airplanes (1870-1914)
  • Led to today: Computers, internet, smartphones - all built on industrial mindset

üåç Environmental Cost

  • Coal burning: Started CO‚ÇÇ emissions ‚Üí climate change
  • Pollution: Rivers, air, soil contaminated
  • Deforestation: Forests cut for fuel, space
  • Still dealing with consequences today!

üí° Lessons for Today

  • ‚öñÔ∏è Progress ‚â† automatic good - technology must serve humanity, not just profit
  • ‚úä Workers' rights matter - laws protect people from exploitation
  • üå± Environmental costs are real - must balance growth with sustainability
  • üéì Education = freedom - why school is your right!
  • üì¢ Change takes activism - factory reforms happened because people fought for them

Chapter 3: Resources & Sustainability ‚Äì Managing Earth's Treasures üåç‚ôªÔ∏è

What Are Natural Resources?

Natural resources are materials or substances found in nature that humans use to survive and build civilizations. Everything you use - your phone, clothes, food, energy - comes from Earth's resources!

üåç Three Main Categories

üå± RENEWABLE

Can be replaced naturally in our lifetime

Examples: Solar, wind, water, forests

‚õΩ NON-RENEWABLE

Once used, GONE forever (or takes millions of years)

Examples: Coal, oil, gas, minerals

‚ôªÔ∏è FLOW RESOURCES

Must be used when available or lost

Examples: Sunlight, wind, tidal energy

üì∫ Watch: Natural Resources Explained

üé• Types of Natural Resources (8 min)

1. Renewable Resources ‚Äì Nature's Gift That Keeps Giving üå±

Renewable resources can regenerate naturally through biological or physical processes. BUT - "renewable" doesn't mean "unlimited"! We can still deplete them through overuse.

‚òÄÔ∏è Solar Energy

  • How it works: Solar panels capture sunlight, convert to electricity
  • Advantages: Unlimited, clean, no emissions, low maintenance
  • Challenges: Expensive setup, only works during daytime, weather-dependent
  • South Africa: Perfect climate! Sun 300+ days/year, huge potential

üí® Wind Energy

  • How it works: Wind spins turbine blades ‚Üí generator creates electricity
  • Advantages: Clean, renewable, low operating cost
  • Challenges: Noisy, bird deaths, needs consistent wind, visual pollution
  • South Africa: Wind farms in Western & Eastern Cape (coastal winds!)

üíß Hydroelectric Power (Water)

  • How it works: Dams hold water, release through turbines ‚Üí electricity
  • Advantages: Reliable, stores energy, flood control, long-lasting
  • Challenges: Expensive dams, displaces communities, ecosystem damage
  • South Africa: Gariep Dam (largest), provides water & electricity

üåæ Biomass (Organic Matter)

  • What it is: Wood, agricultural waste, animal dung burned for energy
  • Advantages: Uses waste, renewable if replanted, available everywhere
  • Challenges: Smoke pollution, deforestation if not managed, lower energy output
  • South Africa: Many rural areas still use wood for cooking

üå≤ Forests & Trees

  • Uses: Timber, paper, medicine, oxygen production, climate regulation
  • Renewable IF: Trees replanted at same rate as cutting (sustainable forestry)
  • Threats: Deforestation faster than regrowth, illegal logging
  • Conservation: Reforestation, protected forests, sustainable logging practices

‚ö†Ô∏è Important:

"Renewable" doesn't mean we can use unlimited amounts! Overfishing = fish populations crash. Over-logging = forests disappear. We must use renewables sustainably (at a rate nature can replace them).

2. Non-Renewable Resources ‚Äì Use Them Wisely! ‚õΩ

Non-renewable resources took MILLIONS of years to form. Once we use them, they're GONE. No second chances!

‚õΩ Fossil Fuels (Coal, Oil, Natural Gas)

ü™® COAL

  • Formation: Dead plants compressed underground for 300+ million years
  • Uses: Electricity generation (power plants), steel production
  • South Africa: 5th largest coal producer globally! Mpumalanga has huge reserves
  • Problems: HUGE CO‚ÇÇ emissions (climate change), air pollution, mining dangers

üõ¢Ô∏è OIL (Petroleum)

  • Formation: Ancient sea organisms compressed for millions of years
  • Uses: Petrol/diesel (cars), plastics, asphalt, medicines, cosmetics
  • South Africa: Limited oil - imports from Middle East
  • Problems: Oil spills destroy ecosystems, CO‚ÇÇ emissions, wars over oil

üî• NATURAL GAS

  • Formation: Same as oil - ancient organisms under pressure
  • Uses: Cooking, heating, electricity
  • South Africa: Some gas off coast, mostly imported
  • Advantage: Burns cleaner than coal/oil (less pollution)
  • Problem: Still produces CO‚ÇÇ, methane leaks (powerful greenhouse gas!)

‚è∞ Running Out: At current rate: Oil ~50 years left, Natural gas ~60 years, Coal ~130 years. Time to switch to renewables!

üíé Minerals & Metals

ü™ô GOLD

South Africa was #1 producer! Witwatersrand Basin has 50% of world's gold. Uses: Jewelry, electronics, currency reserves

üíé DIAMONDS

Kimberley = famous diamond city! Uses: Jewelry, industrial cutting tools (hardest natural material)

ü¶æ IRON ORE

Makes steel (buildings, cars, machinery). Northern Cape has large deposits

‚ö° PLATINUM

South Africa has 80% of world's platinum! Uses: Catalytic converters (cars), jewelry, medical equipment

‚ôªÔ∏è Good News:

Unlike fossil fuels, metals can be RECYCLED! Recycling aluminum saves 95% energy vs. mining new. Recycle your cans!

‚ò¢Ô∏è Nuclear Energy (Uranium)

  • How it works: Splitting uranium atoms releases HUGE energy ‚Üí heats water ‚Üí steam ‚Üí electricity
  • Advantages: No CO‚ÇÇ emissions, very powerful (small fuel = big energy)
  • Disadvantages: Radioactive waste (dangerous for thousands of years), Chernobyl/Fukushima disasters, expensive
  • South Africa: Koeberg Nuclear Power Station (Cape Town) - only nuclear plant in Africa!

‚òÅÔ∏è Chapter 4: Climate & Weather

Understanding Earth's atmospheric patterns and South Africa's diverse climate zones

üå°Ô∏è Introduction: Weather vs. Climate

Many people confuse weather and climate, but they're different time scales of the same atmospheric conditions. Weather is what's happening RIGHT NOW - today's temperature, rainfall, wind. Climate is the AVERAGE pattern over 30+ years - what you can EXPECT in a region during a certain season.

‚òÅÔ∏è WEATHER

Time Scale: Hours to days
Nature: Variable & unpredictable
Example: "It's raining today"
Elements: Temperature, precipitation, wind, humidity, air pressure

üåç CLIMATE

Time Scale: 30+ years
Nature: Predictable patterns
Example: "Cape Town has wet winters"
Factors: Latitude, altitude, ocean currents, distance from sea

üåê Section 1: Earth's Climate Zones

Earth has three main climate zones determined by latitude (distance from the equator). The sun's rays hit different parts of Earth at different angles, creating temperature variations.

üî• TROPICAL (0¬∞-23.5¬∞)

Location: Near equator
Temperature: Hot all year (25-30¬∞C)
Rainfall: High (tropical rainforests)
Examples: Amazon, Congo Basin, Indonesia

üå§Ô∏è TEMPERATE (23.5¬∞-66.5¬∞)

Location: Between tropics and poles
Temperature: Moderate with 4 distinct seasons
Rainfall: Varies (seasonal patterns)
Examples: South Africa, Europe, USA, Australia

‚ùÑÔ∏è POLAR (66.5¬∞-90¬∞)

Location: Near poles
Temperature: Very cold all year (-40¬∞C to 0¬∞C)
Rainfall: Very low (ice deserts)
Examples: Antarctica, Arctic regions

üáøüá¶ Section 2: South Africa's Climate Diversity

South Africa sits in the temperate zone (between 22¬∞S and 35¬∞S latitude), but has HUGE climate variety due to ocean currents, mountains, and altitude differences. We have everything from deserts to subtropical forests!

üåä Ocean Currents Influence:

üî• AGULHAS CURRENT (East Coast)

Warm water from Indian Ocean ‚Üí Makes eastern areas HUMID & HOT ‚Üí Durban, Maputo climate

‚ùÑÔ∏è BENGUELA CURRENT (West Coast)

Cold water from Antarctica ‚Üí Makes western areas DRY & COOL ‚Üí Namib Desert, Cape west coast

‚òÄÔ∏è Major Climate Regions:

üèúÔ∏è 1. DESERT (Northern Cape, Northwest)

Rainfall: < 200mm/year
Temperature: Very hot days (40¬∞C+), cold nights

üåø 2. SEMI-ARID (Karoo, Free State)

Rainfall: 200-600mm/year
Temperature: Hot summers, cold winters (frost common)
Features: Great Karoo, grasslands
Cities: Kimberley, Beaufort West

üåä 3. MEDITERRANEAN (Western Cape)

Rainfall: 500-1000mm/year (WINTER RAIN!)
Temperature: Mild, wet winters; hot, dry summers
Features: Fynbos vegetation, wine regions
Cities: Cape Town, Stellenbosch

‚õàÔ∏è 4. TEMPERATE (Eastern Cape, Gauteng)

Rainfall: 600-1000mm/year (all year round)
Temperature: Warm summers, cool winters
Features: Grasslands, highveld plateau
Cities: Johannesburg, Pretoria, Port Elizabeth

üå¥ 5. SUBTROPICAL (KZN, Mpumalanga)

Rainfall: 1000-1500mm/year (summer thunderstorms)
Temperature: Hot, humid summers; mild winters
Features: Coastal forests, sugarcane farms
Cities: Durban, Nelspruit, Richards Bay

üå°Ô∏è Section 3: Climate Change & Its Impact on South Africa

Climate change is the long-term shift in global temperatures and weather patterns. Since the 1800s, human activities (burning fossil fuels, deforestation) have been the MAIN driver, pumping CO‚ÇÇ and other greenhouse gases into the atmosphere.

üî• The Greenhouse Effect Amplified:

1. Sun's energy enters Earth's atmosphere
2. Earth absorbs some energy and radiates heat back
3. Greenhouse gases (CO‚ÇÇ, methane, nitrous oxide) TRAP heat
4. More gases = MORE trapped heat = GLOBAL WARMING
5. Global average temperature has risen 1.1¬∞C since 1880

üåç Impacts on South Africa:

üíß 1. WATER SCARCITY

‚Ä¢ Reduced rainfall in already dry areas
‚Ä¢ Cape Town "Day Zero" water crisis (2018)
‚Ä¢ More frequent droughts
‚Ä¢ Threatens agriculture & drinking water

üî• 2. EXTREME WEATHER

‚Ä¢ More intense heatwaves (40¬∞C+ days)
‚Ä¢ Severe flooding (KZN floods 2022)
‚Ä¢ Stronger storms & destructive hail
‚Ä¢ Unpredictable growing seasons

üåæ 3. FOOD SECURITY

‚Ä¢ Crop failures from drought/floods
‚Ä¢ Maize production threatened
‚Ä¢ Livestock losses during droughts
‚Ä¢ Food prices increase

üèñÔ∏è 4. COASTAL IMPACTS

‚Ä¢ Rising sea levels (melting ice caps)
‚Ä¢ Coastal erosion (beaches shrinking)
‚Ä¢ Threats to Durban, Cape Town ports
‚Ä¢ Damage to coastal infrastructure

üêò 5. BIODIVERSITY LOSS

‚Ä¢ Species unable to adapt to rapid changes
‚Ä¢ Fynbos biome at risk
‚Ä¢ Coral bleaching (warmer oceans)
‚Ä¢ Wildlife migration patterns disrupted
